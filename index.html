Isulin and Remodulin Pump Disposable

For the past year and a half at DEKA, I worked as a Mechanical Engineer on an Insulin and Remodulin pump disposable. My role was to update the previous cassette which would improve the injection mouldability of the parts per the requests of the injection molding company. To improve usabiity by the patient, I designed next generation cassettes. I also developed and built a variety of fixtures for both testing and constructing the cassettes, including a fixture for measuring the pressure/volume curve of one of the cassette valves which reads changes in volume on the order of microliters when the pressure across the valve is changed.

Reverse Frequency Pendulum Wave Sculpture

As part of a DEKA tradition, the employees at DEKA build an engineering related holiday present for Dean Kamen to display in his house. During 2018, we decided to build a pendulum wave sculpture to be displayed at DEKA. We designed the frame to emulate Manchester's Notre Dame Bridge, which was torn down in 1989 despite Dean's efforts to convince the city to restore it. The pendulums were designed such that after they were all released at once, they would oscillate different frequencies and grow out of sync. After exactly one minute, the pendulums would all line up again in the same position they started. As an extra twist, we used counter-weights to reverse the relative frequencies of the pendulums such that the longer pendulums would oscillate faster than shorter ones.

Delta Drive Sand Table

During my second year at DEKA, I led the holiday gift team to build a Sisyphus style table that drags a ball bearing along the sand inside of the table to draw designs. Our table uses a delta drive system, which controls the positions of three vertical linear carriages. These carriages move the magnet controlling the ball bearing in three dimensions. The table was designed to be in the shape of a Reuleaux triangle in order to maximize the drawing space. We hid the controls for the tables in the leg screws using capacitance sensors to keep the design sleek. New designs can be uploaded into Marlin control software for custom drawings.

Lateral Biopsy Tool

For my senior capstone project, I worked with a team of five students to develop a novel biopsy tool for use in Auris Health's robotic bronchoscope. The tool was designed to be able to articulate laterally with respect to the bronchoscope, allowing the surgeon to take biopsies without having to aim the scope directly at the target. We created the tool from the ground up, brainstorming many ideas and narrowing them down with increasingly higher fidelity prototypes. By the end of the project, we created an articulating needle with three degrees of freedom (radial angle, lateral angle, and insertion depth) controlled by one hand for ease of use. We created a functioning prototype that successfully collected a biopsy sample from a pig cadaver. We also designed a system for integrating our tool into Auris' robotic bronchoscope.

Wind Powered Engine Sculpture

For my final Mechanical Design project, I worked in a team of four to design an interactive wind sculpture that displayed the inner workings of a boxer style six-cylinder engine. The design is a bench in the shape of an engine block with a glass top, allowing people to sit on it and watch the inner workings of the engine. As the wind turbine spins, the engine components move, showing the steps of the auto cycle. There is also a transmission that mimics the car transmission and allows someone to shift gears, slowing down or speeding up the engine.

Pnuematic Robotic Shoulder Joint

I designed, built, and tested a pneumatically actuated shoulder joint. By using soft compressive elements rather than pistons or a McKibben actuator, the size of the joint can be greatly reduced. The joint uses two pouches at opposite sides of the joint which are filled with air to apply a compressive force. When air is added or released in each pouch, the two plates move relative to each other at an angle. An accelerometer gyroscope is used to track the angle of the joint and a PID control system adjusts the air flow into each pouch until the desired angle is achieved. This joint has one degree of freedom, but is designed to be easily modified for two.

Checkers Playing Robot

Working with a team of 5 students, we designed and built a checkers playing robot for under $180. For our mechanical system, we used a three axis gantry to navigate to the desired position and a universal gripper. The gripper utilized a process called jamming in which a balloon filled with coffee grounds is pressed against and forms around an object. Excess air is then sucked out of the balloon causing the coffee grounds to pack around the object and grip it tightly. We used a Raspberry Pi and OpenCV for image processing and an Arduino for motor control. For more information click here to visit our webpage.
